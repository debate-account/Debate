# Sample Round — "This House Would ban animal entertainment"

Cross-examination, then the Proposition 2nd-speaker rebuttal. Side chatter, heckling, and off-topic talk removed; speakers labeled PROP / OPP.

## Cross-examination (back-and-forth)

**OPP:** You propose banning all animal entertainment on the basis of abuse. But wouldn't you agree that also eliminates ethical institutions that protect animals rather than hurt them?

**PROP:** It might, but that ignores how many animals are abused for the sake of so-called entertainment — when we could get the same thing from CGI and other alternatives. Can I ask a question?

**OPP:** It's a back-and-forth? Okay.

**PROP:** What knowledge have these children acquired that they couldn't acquire anywhere else?

**OPP:** As I mentioned, empathy toward animals — it has a broader societal impact as they grow up.

**PROP:** Couldn't they get that somewhere else — reading something, or watching a movie about animals?

**OPP:** Which is more impactful: reading a book, or being in front of animals in their own habitats and learning from them?

**PROP:** Well — it's not a habitat. *(concedes the point)*

**OPP:** Since abuse is already illegal in many countries, wouldn't the solution be better enforcement, not a blanket ban?

**PROP:** It's not illegal in many countries. America hasn't banned it; in Spain, bullfights and greyhound racing are still prominent.

**OPP:** You mentioned CGI — but wouldn't you agree CGI does nothing to house, feed, rehabilitate, or protect actual animals, which ethical institutions do?

**PROP:** According to ncwildlife.org, injured wild animals are often resilient and frequently survive injuries, and many species have a better chance of survival left in their natural habitat. So rehabilitation centers aren't necessarily helping — the added stress and injury can drive very high mortality.

**OPP:** But wouldn't you agree seeing a real animal boosts donations, advocacy, and behavioral change, and that CGI lacks that authenticity?

**PROP:** Not particularly. I have a question too: wouldn't you agree it's better not to prolong a tortured existence?

**OPP:** No — there's a flip side, where animal entertainment can actually benefit animal welfare as a whole, rather than the "horrible existence" you depict.

**PROP:** Then would you rather be enclosed in a 10-by-10 glass cage for the rest of your life? *(time called)*

## Proposition 2nd-speaker rebuttal

My time starts now. First I'll refute their rebuttals and their claim.

First, they claimed animals can heal themselves in the wild — but that's like saying I can grow back a broken bone; it doesn't mean it heals better without a doctor.

They say animals face stress in zoos, but that's non-unique: stress exists in the wild too, with apex predators preying on them. Modern accredited zoos use veterinary and basic medical care to minimize stress, and often reduce net suffering compared to the wild.

They say robotics and CGI are almost indistinguishable from real animals. Even if visually similar, they lack the authenticity that drives donations and care, because people know it's fake. Emotional and behavioral impact comes from real presence — no physical interaction or unpredictability means weaker engagement, and you can't generate the same funding incentives from something that isn't an animal.

They say documentaries make zoos unnecessary for awareness. Also non-unique: documentaries exist alongside zoos, not as replacements. Passive viewing isn't active engagement like visits, donations, and volunteering — and documentaries often rely on zoo footage, so removing zoos collapses a major funding pathway. Engagement mechanisms crumble and funding collapses.

I'd then go to my third contention, which I didn't reach in my first speech — *(time expired)*.
